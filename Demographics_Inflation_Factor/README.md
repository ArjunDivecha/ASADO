# Demographic Inflation Pressure (DIP) Factor — v1

**Built:** May 2026 from UN WPP 2024 cohort data and Juselius & Takats (2018) BIS WP 722 coefficients.

**Purpose:** A slow-moving, demographically-forecastable country score for ASADO. Captures the inflation pressure each country's age structure exerts on its CPI, separated into an absolute level and a cross-country relative measure.

---

## TL;DR

`DIP_rel` is the directly tradeable signal: it is each country's age-structure-driven inflation pressure relative to the panel mean each year. Positive = relatively more inflationary demographics than the panel average. The series is mechanically forecastable to 2050+ because most of the cohort math is already baked in (people aged 60 today will be aged 80 in 2046 with very high probability).

Headline finding from the build:

- **2025 most inflationary demographics:** Korea (+3.1pp), Italy (+2.0), China (+1.9), Portugal, Spain, Greece, Germany.
- **2025 most disinflationary:** South Africa (-4.9), India (-3.7), Malaysia (-3.0), Mexico (-2.9), Indonesia, Turkey.
- **2050 reversal:** Poland (+3.3), Mexico (+2.4), Brazil (+2.0), Chile (+1.9) move to the top. Korea drops to mid-pack as the 80+ cohort's strongly negative coefficient takes over.
- US: peak DIP_abs around 2025 (+3.1pp), declining through 2040s as boomers move into the 80+ bucket.

## Methodology

### Source paper

Juselius, M. and Takats, E. (2018). "The enduring link between demography and inflation." BIS Working Paper No. 722. https://www.bis.org/publ/work722.htm

Key result: in a 22-country, 1870-2016 panel, inflation has a robust, stable relationship to the population age structure. Dependants (young and old) are inflationary; working-age cohorts are disinflationary; the 80+ open-ended cohort is sharply disinflationary (likely a longevity channel through savings).

### Coefficient choice

We use the paper's Table 1 Model 2 specification (with country and time fixed effects). The polynomial coefficients are:

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| gamma_1 | 0.74    | from displayed coefficient on n_tilde_1 (x1)   |
| gamma_2 | -0.183  | from displayed coefficient on n_tilde_2 (x10)  |
| gamma_3 | 0.0168  | from displayed coefficient on n_tilde_3 (x100) |
| gamma_4 | -0.0005 | from displayed coefficient on n_tilde_4 (x1000)|

The 17 cohort coefficients beta_1k are recovered via Appendix B Eq B2:

    beta_1k = gamma_0 + sum_{p=1..4} gamma_p * k^p

with gamma_0 chosen so that sum_k beta_1k = 0 (centering constraint, Eq B5).

The fact that we use the **with-time-FE** coefficients is deliberate. Those coefficients capture the cross-sectional component of demographic pressure — exactly what is useful for a country rotation factor. The **without-time-FE** coefficients in Section 4.1 of the paper give larger magnitudes (the paper's headline 7pp US swing) but include a global trend component that is not what we want to monetize cross-sectionally.

### Two derived signals

For each country j and year t, with cohort shares n_kjt summing to 1:

**Absolute DIP:**

    DIP_abs(j, t) = 100 * sum_k beta_1k * n_kjt

Units: percentage points of inflation. Useful for absolute country-level forecasts but contaminated by the country fixed effect that we cannot estimate without re-running the regression.

**Cross-country relative DIP (the ASADO signal):**

    DIP_rel(j, t) = 100 * sum_k beta_1k * (n_kjt - n_bar_kt)

where n_bar_kt is the panel mean of cohort k in year t. By construction sums to zero across the panel each year. This is the within-year cross-sectional signal that the with-time-FE specification identifies.

**Change signals:**

`DIP_rel_chg_5y` and `DIP_rel_chg_10y` capture demographic regime change over the recent past. These are arguably less priced than the level (which is publicly known and slow-moving) and may be the more tradeable cut.

### Validation

The US `DIP_abs` series reproduces the shape of the paper's Graph 4:

- 1950s trough (~-1.7pp centered)
- Late-1970s peak (+0.8pp): boomers as young dependants
- 1990s trough (-1.1pp): boomers in prime working age, demographic dividend
- 2025 peak (+3.1pp): boomers retiring into the 60-79 cohort (positive coefficient)
- 2040s+ decline: 80+ cohort grows, sharp negative coefficient dominates

Total US swing 1950-2025: ~5pp, of which ~3pp is the cross-sectional component captured by `DIP_rel`.

## Caveats

1. **With-FE coefficients capture cross-sectional component only.** Magnitudes are ~70% of the paper's without-FE estimates. For absolute country-level inflation pressure, the without-FE coefficients should be used (the published version of those is OCR-garbled in the version I worked from; reach for a clean copy if needed).

2. **Paper estimated on advanced economies only.** Applying the same coefficients to the 12 EM additions is extrapolation. EM pension systems, monetary regimes, and informal labor markets differ. Treat EM scores as directionally informative but quantitatively suspect until back-tested against EM inflation.

3. **WPP medium-fertility variant only.** Probabilistic bands are not included. WPP 2024 does provide probabilistic projections — these could be layered in for an uncertainty-weighted version.

4. **100+ cohort collapsed into 80+.** WPP 2024 publishes single-year ages up to 100+, but the paper used 80+ as its top open-ended bucket. We aggregate to match. If you want to preserve the longevity signal more finely (e.g., separating 80-89 from 90+), the polynomial would need re-estimation.

5. **No control variables.** The paper's regression also includes the real interest rate and output gap. The DIP factor here is the demographic-only component; it does not net out concurrent monetary effects.

## Suggested integration with T2 / ASADO

Treat `DIP_rel`, `DIP_rel_chg_5y`, and `DIP_rel_chg_10y` as three independent factor columns in T2:

- **Level (`DIP_rel`):** slow-moving, mostly known to the market. Useful as a structural overlay; may be partially priced.
- **5-year change:** medium-frequency. Captures the demographic regime shift each country is in *now*. Probably the most tradeable cut.
- **10-year change:** similar but with longer history; cleaner for backtest periods.

Orthogonality test: regress `DIP_rel` changes on existing T2 factor returns. Should overlap most with any long-horizon real rate, demographic, or savings-glut factors you already have. Should be orthogonal to momentum, valuation, and GDELT signals.

For the **forward-looking** version, use `DIP_rel(j, t+5)` minus `DIP_rel(j, t)` from the WPP projection. This is information that is technically available today but rarely used systematically.

## How to extend

1. **Universe expansion:** add more ISO3 codes to `ASADO_EM` and rerun.
2. **Probabilistic projections:** swap WPP medium for low/high variants, or load the probabilistic CSV from WPP and propagate uncertainty.
3. **Without-FE coefficients:** if you have clean numbers from Section 4.1 of the paper, swap them in to `GAMMA` and add a second factor variant.
4. **Validation against realized inflation:** load actual CPI for 2017-2025 by country (OECD or IMF), regress 5y/10y forward inflation on `DIP_rel`, check sign and magnitude survive on out-of-sample (COVID-inclusive) data.
5. **Real-rate channel:** the paper's interpretation runs through the natural rate. Use WPP-derived `DIP_rel` as an input to a Laubach-Williams-style r-star model rather than directly to inflation.

## Open questions worth flagging

- The paper's Section 4.1 equation has OCR issues in the version processed for this build. The exact without-FE coefficients are recoverable from a clean copy of the PDF — that would let us add an `absolute` factor variant calibrated to the larger magnitudes.
- Migration-driven age structure changes (e.g., US Hispanic immigration, EU labor mobility) affect the working-age share without going through the lifecycle savings channel in the same way as fertility or longevity. The factor does not distinguish these sources.
- Goodhart-Pradhan (2020) "Great Demographic Reversal" argues this signal should also feed through to real rates and equity risk premia via the global savings glut unwinding. Layering that on would convert this from a country inflation factor to a country equity factor.
